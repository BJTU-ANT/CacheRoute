The mean center of the United States population is a statistical point
calculated by the United States Census Bureau that represents the
geographic average location of all residents in the country at a given
time. This point is determined by treating the population distribution
as if every resident had equal weight and were placed on a flat map, and
then computing the balancing point of that distribution. The concept
differs from the population center, which identifies the point where an
imaginary flat map of the United States would balance if it were
weighted by the population at each location. The mean center instead
reflects a calculated average of latitude and longitude values for the
population. Historically the movement of the mean center of population
has illustrated long term migration and demographic change across the
United States. Since the first census in 1790 the average population
location has gradually shifted westward and slightly southward as
settlement expanded beyond the original Atlantic coast states toward the
interior of the continent. Early measurements placed the center of
population in Maryland, reflecting the concentration of early American
settlements along the eastern seaboard. As transportation networks
improved and new territories were settled the center moved steadily
through Virginia, West Virginia, Kentucky, Indiana, and Missouri. By the
late twentieth and early twenty first centuries the movement of the
center slowed as population growth occurred in multiple regions
including the Midwest, the South, and the western states. The Census
Bureau continues to calculate the mean center after each decennial
census in order to analyze demographic trends and understand the
geographic distribution of residents. In recent decades population
growth in the Sun Belt, particularly in states such as Texas, Florida,
Arizona, and Nevada, has contributed to subtle shifts in the national
population balance. At the same time major metropolitan regions across
the country continue to influence the distribution of population because
of economic opportunities, immigration patterns, and regional migration.
Researchers and geographers often study the movement of the mean center
of population to illustrate how settlement patterns, industrialization,
urbanization, and economic development have shaped the geography of the
United States over time. The data also provide insight into long term
demographic transitions such as suburban expansion, internal migration,
and the growth of large metropolitan corridors across the country. The
